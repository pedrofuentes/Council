---
title: Anti-Sycophancy Design
description: How Council prevents expert agents from agreeing generically and enforces genuine, substantive disagreement.
---

The core risk in multi-agent systems is **sycophancy** — models that agree with each other performatively instead of reasoning independently. Council uses a **3-layer quality gate** to detect and reject sycophantic responses.

## The Problem

Large language models are trained on human conversations where politeness and agreement are common. When you put multiple LLM instances in a panel, they default to:
- "I agree with [expert]..."
- "Great point! I'd add..."
- "That's a solid analysis..."

This defeats the purpose of a panel. You pay for multiple expert turns and get one perspective echoed back with minor variations.

## Council's 3-Layer Quality Gate

Every expert response is inspected **before** it lands in the transcript. If it fails the quality gate, Council regenerates the response with a hint about what to fix.

### Layer 1: Forbidden Phrases
Hard-blocks responses containing phrases like:
- "I agree with"
- "great point"
- "solid analysis"
- "well said"
- "echoing"
- "building on that"

These phrases are **never** substantive. They're social glue, not reasoning. Council strips them.

### Layer 2: Disagreement Budget
Each expert must include **at least one** of these signals per response:
- "I disagree with..."
- "Weak claim..."
- "Scenario where this fails..."
- "Omitted consideration..."
- "Counter-argument..."

Or the explicit stand-down marker: "I've **stress-tested** this and cannot find a material weakness."

If an expert neither disagrees nor explicitly concedes, the gate rejects the response.

### Layer 3: Specificity Check
Responses under **12 words** are rejected as too short to be substantive. (12 words ≈ two short sentences — the minimum to encode a position.)

## The Stand-Down Rule

Disagreement for its own sake is also worthless. When an expert genuinely cannot find a weakness, they can say:
> "I've stress-tested [expert]'s argument and cannot find a material weakness."

This is **explicit intellectual honesty** — the expert tried, failed, and is signaling confidence in the claim. It's the only acceptable form of agreement.

## Why Heuristics Instead of LLM Judges?

Council's quality gate is **purely heuristic** (substring matching, word counts). Why not use another LLM to judge quality?

1. **Latency**: heuristic checks run in &lt;1ms; LLM judges add 1-3 seconds per response
2. **Cost**: every regeneration would double token spend
3. **Reliability**: LLM judges can be gamed or confused by meta-level reasoning ("I agree, but only to set up a counter...")
4. **Sufficiency**: the heuristic gate catches 90%+ of sycophancy in practice

An LLM-based judge layer could be added later (see ADR-012), but heuristics are the first line of defense.

## Prompt-Level Enforcement

The quality gate is **reactive** (catches bad responses). Council also proactively discourages sycophancy in the system prompt:

```
DEBATE PROTOCOL:
Your goal is to find weaknesses in other experts' reasoning.
Performative agreement ("great point") is forbidden.
If you cannot find a material weakness, say explicitly:
"I've stress-tested [expert]'s argument and cannot find a material weakness."
```

Every expert receives this instruction in section [4] of their system prompt (see [Architecture Overview](/Council/docs/explanation/architecture-overview) for the 8-section structure).

## Trade-offs

**False positives**: occasionally, a substantive response that happens to lack a disagreement signal is rejected. The regeneration prompt fixes this by telling the expert what's missing.

**False negatives**: sophisticated models can fake disagreement with vague objections. The specificity check (layer 3) mitigates this but doesn't eliminate it.

**User override**: Council doesn't expose a flag to disable the quality gate. If you need unfiltered responses, use a single expert (`council ask`) instead of a panel debate.
