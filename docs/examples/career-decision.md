# Career Decision: Individual Contributor to Engineering Manager

> **Note:** This is an illustrative example transcript showing the output shape of a `council convene` session followed by `council conclude` and `council export`. It demonstrates how Council's expert panel deliberates on a high-stakes personal career decision.

## Decision Prompt

"Should I take the engineering manager role my director offered? I'm a senior IC with 7 years of experience, love coding, and have mentored 3 junior engineers. Salary bump is 15%, but I'd manage a team of 6."

## Panel Roster

- **Maya Okoye (IC Mentor)** — Principal engineer with 15 years as an IC; advocates for the depth and impact possible without management
- **Thomas Brennan (Engineering Manager)** — Former senior IC who made the switch 4 years ago; pragmatic about the transition's realities
- **Linda Cho (VP Engineering)** — Leads 40 engineers across 6 teams; understands organizational dynamics and long-term career paths
- **Dr. Aisha Patel (Career Coach)** — Specializes in tech career transitions; focused on self-awareness and decision frameworks over advice

---

## Round 1: Initial Positions

### Maya Okoye (IC Mentor)

Before you even consider this, ask yourself: **why are you being offered this role?** Is it because you've demonstrated exceptional people leadership, or because you're the "least-bad" option after the previous manager left? I've seen too many strong ICs pressured into management because the company needs a warm body, not because the role aligns with their strengths.

You said you "love coding." That phrase should set off alarms. Engineering management is **not** "coding + people management." It's people management, and coding becomes a luxury you squeeze in after hours if you're lucky. If you take this role expecting to stay technical, you'll fail at both—your code quality will slip, and your reports won't get the attention they need. The 15% salary bump is not compensation for losing something you love; it's a down payment on career regret.

### Thomas Brennan (Engineering Manager)

Maya's painting a dire picture, but she's not wrong about the coding tradeoff. I write code maybe 10% of my time now—bug fixes, PR reviews, and the occasional spike when the team is blocked. The rest is: 1:1s, sprint planning, performance reviews, hiring, cross-team coordination, and putting out fires. It's a **different job**, and you need to be honest about whether that energizes or drains you.

That said, Maya's framing assumes management is a consolation prize. It's not. The leverage is real: when you're an IC, your impact is bounded by your own output. As a manager, you multiply through six people. If you coach someone through a technical decision that saves 2 weeks of work, that's **your** impact even if you didn't write the code. The question isn't "do I love coding"—it's "do I get more satisfaction from building things or building people?"

### Linda Cho (VP Engineering)

Let me add organizational context you're not seeing. Your company is offering you this role at 7 years of experience, which is **early** by industry standards. Most companies promote ICs to management at 9–12 years. That's either a signal that you're exceptionally ready, or a signal that the company is desperate and willing to roll the dice on an underprepared manager.

Here's the blunt reality: if you take this role and struggle, the company won't demote you back to IC—they'll performance-manage you out. Management roles have higher visibility and higher stakes. A senior IC who ships slowly is a local problem; a manager who loses team trust is an organizational problem. You need to ask your director: "What does success look like in the first 90 days?" If the answer is vague, that's a red flag.

### Dr. Aisha Patel (Career Coach)

Everyone here is giving you **their** lens, but this decision is about **your** values and your definition of impact. Let's reframe: instead of "should I take this role," ask "what am I optimizing for in the next 3 years?"

- If you're optimizing for **technical depth** (becoming a domain expert, conference talks, open-source contributions), management is a detour. You'll lose 2–3 years of technical growth that's hard to recover.
- If you're optimizing for **organizational influence** (shaping team culture, hiring, strategic decisions), IC paths hit a ceiling. You can become a Staff+ IC, but your influence radius is smaller than a manager's.
- If you're optimizing for **compensation**, both paths top out similarly at senior levels—Staff ICs and Directors earn comparable salaries in most companies.

The 15% bump is irrelevant. What matters is: when you imagine your day-to-day in 2 years, which version of yourself is more energized—the one in flow state writing code, or the one in back-to-back 1:1s helping reports navigate challenges?

## Round 2: Direct Challenges

### Maya Okoye (IC Mentor)

Thomas, your "building people vs. building things" framing is a false binary. I've mentored 12 engineers over my career, led design reviews that shaped the architecture of our entire platform, and unblocked teams through technical spikes—all without a management title. The idea that you need formal authority to have people impact is **organizationally lazy**. Companies should invest in IC leadership paths, not funnel every senior engineer into management.

And Linda, you're right that this is an early promotion, but your "higher visibility = higher stakes" logic cuts both ways. If the OP stays an IC and **doesn't** ship impact, they'll hit a career plateau. At least as a manager, they're getting exposure to the strategic layer of the organization. Visibility is risk, but it's also opportunity.

### Thomas Brennan (Engineering Manager)

Maya, you're conflating "mentorship" with "management." Mentoring 12 engineers over a career is 1 engineer per year on average. I'm responsible for **six people's performance reviews, career development, compensation, and happiness** right now, this quarter. The accountability is different. When one of my reports is underperforming, I can't hand-wave it as "they didn't take my advice"—it's **my** problem to solve, and if I don't, my director holds **me** accountable.

Aisha, I agree the "energized" framing is useful, but it's too abstract. The OP needs to test the hypothesis. I'd recommend: ask to **shadow** your director for 2 weeks. Sit in on their 1:1s, sprint planning, and incident reviews. If you find yourself thinking, "I could do this better," that's a green light. If you find yourself thinking, "I'd rather be writing code than having this conversation," that's your answer.

### Linda Cho (VP Engineering)

Maya, your "IC leadership path" argument works at Netflix or Google with mature Staff+ tracks. But at most companies, the IC track is a **politeness fiction**. After Senior, the career ladder narrows dramatically—there are 10 senior IC roles for every 1 Staff IC role. If the OP wants long-term career optionality, management is the pragmatic move. You can always **return** to IC later; companies are more hesitant to give someone their first management role at age 40.

Thomas, I like the shadowing idea, but it's incomplete. Shadowing shows you the job on a **good day**. The OP should also talk to: (1) a manager who regrets the switch, (2) a manager who loves it, and (3) an IC who turned down a management role. Triangulating those perspectives gives a fuller picture than any one advisor can provide.

### Dr. Aisha Patel (Career Coach)

Linda, your "politeness fiction" framing is cynical but accurate at many companies. But the OP should interrogate **why** they're at this company. If the company doesn't invest in IC growth, that's a company problem, not a career problem. Taking a management role to navigate a broken promotion system is a Band-Aid, not a strategy.

Here's the uncomfortable question: is the 15% salary bump driving this decision more than you're admitting? If you turned down the role and stayed an IC, would you feel like you "left money on the table"? If so, that's a values clarification exercise—are you optimizing for short-term compensation or long-term fulfillment? Because management salaries plateau too, and if you're miserable in the role, the extra $15K won't offset the Sunday-night dread.

## Synthesis

The panel exposes a tension: the **structural pull** toward management (company needs, career optionality, compensation) versus the **intrinsic pull** toward IC work (technical depth, coding satisfaction, autonomy). The decision quality depends on the OP's **self-awareness** about which tradeoffs they can tolerate.

### Key Insights

1. **Management is not "IC + people"—it's a **different job\*\* that requires letting go of technical control. If coding is identity-level important, management will feel like loss, not growth.
2. **The 7-year promotion timing is a yellow flag.** Either the OP is exceptionally ready, or the company is desperate. Clarify with the director: "What made you confident I'm ready for this now?"
3. **IC vs. manager is not one-way.** The OP can try management for 18 months and return to IC if it's not a fit—but only if they **communicate that possibility upfront** and maintain technical credibility. Letting skills atrophy makes the return path harder.
4. **The "15% bump" is a distraction.** Optimize for 5-year career satisfaction, not 1-year salary delta. Staff ICs and mid-level managers earn similarly in most markets.

### Recommended Decision Framework

Instead of "yes" or "no," the OP should **negotiate a trial period**:

1. **Propose a 6-month "acting manager" role** with explicit checkpoints at 3 and 6 months. Success criteria: team engagement scores, delivery velocity, and personal satisfaction. If any metric is red, revert to IC role with no stigma.
2. **Maintain technical engagement during the trial.** Block 10% of time for code contributions—not to "stay relevant," but to test whether coding 10% feels like a treat or a frustration. That's your barometer.
3. **Identify a "manager mentor"** outside your reporting chain who made the IC→manager switch successfully. Monthly check-ins to process challenges and recalibrate expectations.
4. **Shadow 3 people**: a happy manager, a regretful manager, and an IC who declined management. Synthesize their stories into your own decision criteria.

### What the OP Should **Not** Do

- Accept the role to "try it out" without communicating the trial framing. That sets up misaligned expectations.
- Accept the role primarily for the 15% bump if coding is identity-level important. The compensation won't compensate.
- Decline the role because "I love coding" without testing whether the love is for **coding specifically** or for **solving problems** (which management also involves).

## Next Actions

1. **This week:** Schedule a 1:1 with your director. Ask: "What specific behaviors or outcomes made you think I'm ready for this role?" and "What does success look like in the first 90 days?" If the answers are vague, that's a data point.
2. **Next 2 weeks:** Shadow your director's calendar for 10 hours. Track your emotional response—energized, neutral, or drained—after each activity (1:1s, planning meetings, incident reviews).
3. **Weeks 3–4:** Interview 3 people (happy manager, regretful manager, IC who declined). Ask: "What surprised you about the role?" and "What would you do differently?"
4. **Week 4 decision point:** If the data says "yes," negotiate a 6-month trial with explicit success criteria. If the data says "no," communicate to your director: "I'm not ready yet, but here's what would make me ready in 18 months" (e.g., managing 1–2 interns, leading a cross-team initiative).
5. **If you decline:** Clarify your IC career goals with your director. Ask: "What does the path to Staff IC look like here, and what would I need to demonstrate?" If the answer is unsatisfying, that's a signal about the company's IC investment, not your potential.
